About

I am a Ph.D. student in Electrical Engineering at the University of Texas at Arlington . My research focuses on brain-inspired neuromorphic computing, in-memory computing based on emerging devices, hardware acceleration, and device/circuit modeling. I am particularly interested in memristor-based accelerators, analog neuromorphic computing hardware, and spiking neural networks.

Before joining UTA, I received my M.S. degree in Electrical Engineering from the University of Southern California . and my B.Eng. degree in Automation from Hebei University of Technology . After my undergraduate study, I first worked as an Electrical Engineering Intern at COFCO Coca-Cola, where hands-on experience with industrial electrical equipment made me curious about how hardware systems work internally. Later, during my Software Internship at NXP Semiconductors , I realized that although software is powerful, I was more interested in the underlying devices, circuits, and hardware architectures that make computation possible. These experiences motivated me to pursue integrated circuits and hardware-oriented research, especially in neuromorphic computing, in-memory computing, and accelerator design.

Research

Compact and Energy-Efficient Memristive Spiking Neuromorphic Accelerator for Bio-inspired Interception Tasks

Qianhou Qu, Sheng Lu, Sungyong Jung, Qilian Liang, and Chenyun Pan

Paper Under Review

This work presents a compact and energy-efficient memristive spiking neuromorphic accelerator for bio-inspired interception tasks. The design maps a 4-30-1 spiking neural network onto 1T1R memristor crossbars for in-memory synaptic computation, while a separated input and membrane-node integrate-and-fire neuron is developed to improve temporal integration fidelity during array-interfaced operation. The accelerator is implemented and evaluated at the circuit level using the SkyWater SKY130 PDK, demonstrating reliable spike-based inference, strong agreement with the software SNN baseline, and efficient real-time interception performance.

Memristor-Based Spiking Neural Network Accelerator for Bio-inspired Interception Task

Qianhou Qu, Sheng Lu, Liuting Shang, Jaihan Utailawon, Sungyong Jung, Qilian Liang, and Chenyun Pan

Paper 19th IEEE Dallas Circuits and Systems Conference (DCAS 2026)

This work presents an analog memristor-based spiking neural network accelerator for a bio-inspired interception task. The accelerator integrates memristor crossbar-based in-memory synaptic computation with analog integrate-and-fire neuron circuits to support asynchronous event-driven inference. The system is evaluated on a predator-prey tracking task, where the circuit-level inference closely matches ideal software inference. Compared with a 5 nm digital SNN accelerator baseline, the proposed analog design demonstrates lower energy consumption and reduced delay, showing the potential of memristor-based neuromorphic hardware for efficient real-time edge intelligence.

Memristor-Based Spiking Neural Network Accelerator for Neuromorphic Computing

Qianhou Qu and Chenyun Pan

Paper 2026 Energy Consequences of Information Workshop

This work develops a memristor-based spiking neural network accelerator for reinforcement learning driven neuromorphic computing. A reinforcement learning framework is used to train the spiking neural network for task-oriented decision making, while the trained synaptic weights are mapped onto memristor crossbars and analog integrate-and-fire neuron circuits. The design demonstrates how reinforcement learning, spiking neural dynamics, and in-memory analog computing can be integrated to build efficient neuromorphic accelerators with advantages in area, delay, and energy consumption.

Variation-Aware Memristor-Based Analog Accelerator for Vision Transformer

Qianhou Qu1, Sheng Lu1, Liuting Shang1, Sungyong Jung2, Qilian Liang1, and Chenyun Pan1,*

Paper1: MDPI Electronics, Paper2: 2025 IEEE International Midwest Symposium on Circuits and Systems (MWSCAS)

This work presents a variation-aware memristor-based analog accelerator for Vision Transformer inference. It uses analog in-memory computing to accelerate matrix operations and evaluates the design from the perspectives of area, delay, energy, and device-level variation. The study focuses on improving hardware efficiency for Transformer workloads while considering practical non-idealities in emerging-device-based analog computing systems.
